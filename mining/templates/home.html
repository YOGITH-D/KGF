What is iron ore?

Iron ores are rocks and minerals from which metallic iron can be extracted. There are four main types of iron ore deposit: massive hematite, which is the most commonly mined, magnetite, titanomagnetite, and pisolitic ironstone. These ores vary in colour from dark grey, through to deep purple, rusty red and bright yellow. Iron is responsible for the red colour in many of our rocks and the deep red sands of the Australian deserts.

Hematite contains about 70% iron and is the most widely used ore in steelmaking. It appears reddish-brown and is valued for its high iron content.

Hematite

Magnetite has about 72% iron, the highest among iron ores. It is black, strongly magnetic, and a rich source of iron, though harder to process.

Magnetite

Limonite contains around 40–60% iron. It is brownish and mainly composed of hydrated iron oxides, making it a lower-grade ore.

Limonite

Siderite has about 48% iron and is yellowish-brown. Due to its impurities and lower grade, it is less used in modern steel production.

Siderite

Iron Ore Impurities

Iron ores often contain impurities such as silica, alumina, sulphur, and phosphorus, which lower the ore grade and reduce steelmaking efficiency. Silica is one of the most common impurities, causing higher slag formation during smelting and raising fuel costs. Froth flotation is commonly used to remove silica and concentrate the ore. Clean iron ore is important as it improves steel quality, enhances furnace efficiency, reduces energy use, and ensures better production control.

Froth Floatation

Froth flotation is a widely used method to concentrate iron ore by separating impurities such as silica, which lowers ore quality and increases production costs. Its importance lies in providing cleaner ore, improving steel strength, reducing energy consumption, and ensuring efficient furnace operation. To see the process in detail, watch the video provided.
